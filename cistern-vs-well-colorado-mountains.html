The Mountain Water Dilemma

Living in the beautiful Colorado mountains offers breathtaking views and a serene lifestyle, but it also comes with its own set of challenges. One of the most pressing issues for mountain property owners is the lack of access to municipal water. Unlike urban areas where water is readily available, many mountainous regions are off the grid regarding public water supply. This lack of municipal water forces property owners to consider alternative solutions. The two main options for sourcing water in these remote locations are drilling a well or installing a cistern.

Both options have their advantages and disadvantages, making it essential for homeowners to evaluate their specific situations before making a decision. Factors such as budget, property characteristics, and intended use of the property can influence which option is best for you. In this comprehensive guide, weâ€™ll explore the pros and cons of both wells and cisterns, and offer a side-by-side comparison to help you make an informed choice.

Option 1: Drilling a Well

Drilling a well is often considered the traditional solution for obtaining water in rural and mountainous areas. This option can provide a reliable and seemingly endless supply of water, but it does come with its own set of challenges.

Pros:

  • Unlimited Water Supply: Once a well is successfully drilled, it can offer an ongoing supply of water, which is ideal for both daily use and emergency situations.
  • No Ongoing Water Delivery Costs: Unlike cisterns that require regular water delivery, wells provide water without these recurring expenses.
  • Increases Property Value: Properties with wells can be more appealing to potential buyers, enhancing resale value.
  • Set It and Forget It: Once installed, wells require minimal day-to-day management, allowing homeowners to focus on enjoying their property.

Cons:

  • High Upfront Cost: Drilling a well can be a significant financial investment, often ranging from $15,000 to $50,000 or more in mountainous regions, depending on depth and geological challenges.
  • No Guarantee of Finding Water: There is always a risk that drilling may not yield sufficient water, leading to a wasted investment.
  • Rocky Terrain Equals Expensive Drilling: The rugged landscape of Colorado mountains can complicate the drilling process, increasing costs.
  • Pump Maintenance Required: Wells require regular maintenance for the pump and filtration system to ensure clean and reliable water.
  • Electricity Required: Most wells rely on electric pumps, which adds to the overall operational costs.
  • Can Go Dry in Drought: In times of drought, wells may not produce sufficient water, which could lead to serious shortages.

Best for:

Drilling a well is best suited for properties with a good water table, homeowners who have an adequate budget for potential uncertainties, and those planning to reside in the area long-term.

Option 2: Cistern Installation

For many mountain properties, cisterns offer a viable alternative to wells. Cisterns are storage tanks that hold water, which can be delivered from an external source. This option can provide a reliable water supply without some of the complications associated with drilling a well.

Pros:

  • Predictable, Known Cost: The upfront cost of installing a cistern typically ranges from $8,000 to $20,000, making it generally more affordable than drilling a well.
  • Works Anywhere: Cisterns can be installed regardless of the water table, making them suitable for virtually any property.
  • Can Be Installed Quickly: The installation process for cisterns is often faster than drilling a well, allowing homeowners to gain access to water sooner.
  • No Electricity Needed for Storage: Cisterns do not require electricity for water storage, reducing operational costs.
  • Known Water Quality: Homeowners have control over the quality of the water sourced for the cistern, allowing for more assurance in water safety.
  • Expandable: Cistern systems can be expanded to accommodate increasing water needs, providing flexibility for homeowners.

Cons:

  • Ongoing Water Delivery Costs: Homeowners must budget for the regular delivery of water to fill the cistern, which can add up over time.
  • Requires Monitoring Water Level: Cisterns require regular monitoring to ensure that water levels remain adequate for household needs.
  • Storage Capacity Limits: The size of the cistern dictates how much water can be stored, which may be a concern for larger households or during peak usage times.
  • Delivery Access Needed: Cisterns require access for water delivery trucks, which can be a challenge in difficult terrains or during adverse weather conditions.

Best for:

Cisterns are ideal for properties with a poor water table, homeowners who prefer predictable costs, vacation homes that may not require a continuous water supply, or properties located on challenging terrains.

How to Decide: 5 Questions to Ask

When it comes to making the decision between a well and a cistern, consider asking yourself the following questions:

  1. What's the water table depth on my property? Understanding the local geology is crucial in determining whether a well is a feasible option.
  2. Is this a primary or vacation home? The intended use of the property can greatly influence your water needs and budget.
  3. What's my budget for upfront costs? Assessing your financial situation will help you determine which option is more viable for you.
  4. How do I feel about ongoing water delivery? Consider whether you're comfortable with the logistics and costs associated with water delivery for a cistern.
  5. What did my neighbors do? Gaining insights from neighbors who have made similar decisions can provide valuable context and guidance.
