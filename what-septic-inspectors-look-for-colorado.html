What Septic Inspectors Look For in Colorado: Pass Your Inspection

By Kyle Rossignol

Why Inspections Matter

Septic inspections are critical for safeguarding your investment, ensuring that your system operates efficiently, and protecting the environment. A thorough inspection not only identifies current issues but also prevents future problems that could arise from neglect or lack of maintenance. For homeowners, a functional septic system is a necessity; failing to maintain this system can lead to costly repairs or health hazards, not to mention the potential impact on property value.

In Colorado, septic inspections are often required for obtaining permits related to property transactions or building projects. If you don‚Äôt pass your inspection, you may face delays in closing a sale, be required to make expensive repairs, or even be forced to replace your entire septic system. Understanding what inspectors look for can help you prepare effectively and ensure a smooth process.

What Inspectors Check

During a septic inspection, professionals meticulously evaluate several key components of your system to ensure compliance with local regulations and optimal functionality. Here are the most critical elements they examine:

  • Septic Tank Condition: Inspectors assess the tank for structural integrity, including cracks or leaks. A damaged tank can lead to contamination of groundwater and is a significant red flag.
  • Effluent Filter: This component prevents solids from entering the drain field. Inspectors will check if it‚Äôs clogged or needs cleaning, as a blocked filter can lead to system failures.
  • Drain Field Health: The drain field is crucial for dispersing treated wastewater. Inspectors look for signs of saturation, foul odors, or lush vegetation, which can indicate a failing drainage system.
  • Pumping Records: Having a documented history of regular pumping and maintenance shows diligence in upkeep. Inspectors will want to see that your tank has been pumped at appropriate intervals.
  • Location and Accessibility: Proper placement is essential for drainage and compliance. Inspectors will check if the system is located too close to water sources or property lines, which could violate local regulations.
  • Ventilation: Adequate ventilation is necessary for a septic system to function properly. Inspectors will check if vents are blocked or improperly installed, which can lead to pressure build-up and system failure.
  • Soil Quality: The type of soil around your drain field affects absorption rates. Inspectors may conduct soil tests to ensure it can adequately filter wastewater.
  • System Design: Inspectors will verify that the system was designed and installed according to local codes. Any deviations can lead to potential failures and may require costly modifications.
  • Alarm Systems: If your septic system has a pump, it should have an alarm system to notify you of malfunctions. Inspectors will check if this system is functioning properly.

Common Reasons for Failure

Understanding common pitfalls can help you avoid inspection failures. Here are some frequent issues that arise during inspections:

  • Neglected Pumping: Failing to pump your septic tank regularly can lead to sludge buildup, causing system malfunction. To avoid this, adhere to a pumping schedule, typically every 3-5 years.
  • Improper Waste Disposal: Flushing non-biodegradable items or chemicals can damage your septic system. Educate everyone in your household about what can and cannot go down the drain.
  • Drain Field Saturation: If your drain field is too wet, it can lead to backup and failure. Maintaining proper landscaping and avoiding excess water runoff can help keep your drain field functioning well.
  • Structural Damage: Cracks in the septic tank or lines can lead to leaks. Regular visual inspections and maintenance can spot these issues before they escalate.
  • Blocked Vents or Filters: Clogged filters can lead to system backups. Regular cleaning and inspection of these components are crucial for maintaining system health.

How to Prepare

Preparing for a septic inspection involves several steps to ensure everything goes smoothly. Here‚Äôs what you should have ready:

  • Documentation of previous inspections, pumping records, and maintenance history.
  • Access to all components of the septic system, including the tank and drain field.
  • Clear the area around the septic system; inspectors need unobstructed access to perform a thorough evaluation.

Before the inspection, don‚Äôt hesitate to ask questions. Inquire about what the inspector will be looking for, how long the inspection will take, and whether there are any specific concerns you should address beforehand. Being proactive can set you up for success.
