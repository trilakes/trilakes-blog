First: Don't Panic

We understand that discovering your Perc Test has failed can be incredibly stressful. Take a deep breath and remind yourself that this is a common issue many homeowners face. The most important thing right now is to stay calm and focused. There are several options available to help you navigate this situation effectively.

Your first step is to gather information about your septic system and the specifics of the failed test. Remember, this is not the end of the world; it's a momentary setback. Many homeowners have successfully resolved similar issues by following appropriate steps and seeking assistance when needed. You're not alone in this, and there are resources available to help you through it.

Common Causes

  • Soil Saturation: Heavy rains or snowmelt can saturate the soil around your septic system, leading to a failed Perc Test. Wet soil cannot absorb water efficiently, which can affect the performance of your system.
  • Soil Composition: The type of soil in your area plays a significant role in how well water can percolate. Clay soil, for example, has small particles that restrict water flow, making it difficult for effluent to disperse.
  • System Overload: If your household has increased water usage or if additional people have moved in, your septic system may be overwhelmed, leading to failures in water absorption and treatment.
  • Debris or Clogs: Roots, debris, or other materials can clog the drainage field, resulting in poor absorption rates during the Perc Test.
  • Maintenance Neglect: Failing to pump your septic tank regularly can lead to sludge buildup, which can affect how well the system functions and ultimately result in a test failure.

Immediate Steps to Take

  1. Assess the Situation: Review the details of the Perc Test and any notes provided by the inspector. Identify the reason for the failure if possible.
  2. Limit Water Usage: Reduce water usage in your home to prevent further stress on the system. Avoid laundry, long showers, or running dishwashers.
  3. Inspect Your Property: Look for visible signs of septic failure, like standing water, unpleasant odors, or slow drains. Document these observations.
  4. Contact Your Local Health Department: They can provide guidance on compliance issues and may have resources available to assist you.
  5. Research Your Options: Familiarize yourself with potential solutions, which may include soil remediation, system replacement, or alternative septic systems.
