What is Septic System Installation?

Septic system installation is the process of setting up an onsite wastewater treatment system to manage and treat sewage from homes that are not connected to a centralized sewer system. This is particularly important in areas like Colorado's mountain communities, where homes are often situated far from municipal services. A typical septic system consists of a septic tank and a drain field (also known as a leach field), designed to treat and disperse wastewater safely into the environment.

The septic tank serves as the primary holding chamber where solid waste settles to the bottom, forming sludge, while lighter materials like grease float to the top, creating a scum layer. The wastewater in between is then released into the drain field, where it percolates through soil layers that naturally filter out contaminants. The proper installation of a septic system is crucial for maintaining public health and protecting local water resources.

In Colorado, septic systems must be designed and installed according to state and local regulations, which vary by county. Homeowners must work with licensed septic contractors who understand these regulations and can provide the expertise necessary for successful installation. The Septic System Installation Process: Step by Step

The installation of a septic system involves several critical steps, each requiring careful planning and execution. Hereâ€™s a step-by-step guide to what homeowners can expect during the installation process:

  1. Initial Consultation: Contact a licensed septic contractor to discuss your needs, assess your property, and understand your options.
  2. Site Evaluation: The contractor will perform a comprehensive site evaluation, including soil testing and determining the best location for the septic system.
  3. Perc Test: A perc test (percolation test) is conducted to assess soil absorption rates, which is essential for designing the septic system.
  4. Design Plan: Based on the evaluations, the contractor will create a septic system design that complies with local regulations.
  5. Obtain Permits: All necessary permits must be secured before installation can begin. This process can vary significantly by county.
  6. Excavation: Once permits are obtained, excavation will commence to create space for the septic tank and drain field.
  7. Installation of the Septic Tank: The septic tank is placed into the excavated area, and connections to the homeâ€™s plumbing are made.
  8. Drain Field Installation: The drain field is constructed according to the design plan, ensuring proper spacing and depth for effective wastewater treatment.
  9. Backfill and Grading: After the system is installed, the area is backfilled with soil, and the site is graded to prevent water pooling.
  10. Final Inspection: A final inspection is performed by local health officials to ensure the system meets all regulations and is functioning correctly.
  11. System Education: The contractor will provide information on how to maintain the system and what to avoid to prolong its lifespan.

By understanding these steps, homeowners can better prepare for the installation process and ensure they have a functioning septic system that meets their needs.

Colorado Mountain Construction Challenges

Installing septic systems in Colorado's mountain communities presents unique challenges that require specialized knowledge and expertise. Here are some of the key factors that must be considered:

  • Elevation Considerations: Many mountain properties are located at elevations ranging from 7,000 to 12,000 feet, which can impact soil types, water table levels, and septic system performance.
  • Soil Types: Colorado's diverse geology includes granite, clay, and rocky soils. Each type presents different challenges for septic system design and installation, requiring thorough soil testing to determine the best approach.
  • Weather Windows: The construction season in mountain areas is often limited to May through October, as snow and frost can hinder installation efforts outside this timeframe.
  • Frost Line Depth: In Colorado, the frost line typically reaches depths of 36 to 48 inches, requiring careful planning to ensure the septic system is installed below this level to prevent freezing.
  • Access Issues: Many mountain properties have challenging access roads, steep grades, and limited space, which may require specialized equipment and techniques to complete the installation.

Permits & Regulations by County

When installing a septic system in Colorado, obtaining the necessary permits is essential. Each county has its own regulations governing onsite wastewater treatment systems (OWTS), which dictate design, installation, and maintenance requirements. Below is an overview of the permitting process in several Colorado counties:

  • Teller County: Requires a site evaluation and a permit application submitted to the Environmental Health Department.
  • Park County: Similar to Teller, Park County mandates a site evaluation and specific design criteria based on soil type.
  • El Paso County: Requires detailed documentation, including a perc test and design plans, for approval.
  • Chaffee County: Has specific guidelines for high-elevation installations, with a focus on environmental protection.
  • Summit County: Inspections are crucial here, with strict adherence to design and installation regulations due to high population density.
  • Fremont County: Requires a comprehensive permit application and site evaluation, particularly for areas with challenging soil conditions.
  • Douglas County: Has a robust permitting process that emphasizes sustainability and environmental impact.
