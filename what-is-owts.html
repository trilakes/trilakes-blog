What Is OWTS? On-Site Wastewater Treatment Systems Explained

By Kyle Rossignol, Licensed GC
```html

Quick Answer

OWTS, or On-Site Wastewater Treatment Systems, are systems designed to treat wastewater from homes or businesses that are not connected to a central sewer system. These systems are essential for managing and treating sewage and gray water on-site, ensuring environmental protection and compliance with local regulations.

Detailed Explanation

On-Site Wastewater Treatment Systems (OWTS) are crucial for areas without access to municipal sewer systems, often found in rural or mountainous regions like Colorado. These systems help manage waste generated from kitchens, bathrooms, and laundry facilities, treating it so it can be safely released into the environment or reused. Understanding how OWTS works is vital for homeowners and builders alike.

Typically, an OWTS consists of:

  • Septic Tank: A watertight container that holds sewage for a period, allowing solids to settle at the bottom and liquids to flow out for further treatment.
  • Drainfield: A network of perforated pipes buried in soil that disperses the treated wastewater, allowing it to be absorbed by the ground.
  • Soil Treatment: The natural filtration process where soil microorganisms break down remaining contaminants in the wastewater.

In Colorado, OWTS are necessary for properties that are not connected to city sewer lines, particularly in more remote or mountainous areas. They offer a sustainable way to handle waste while protecting local waterways and groundwater from pollution. Additionally, OWTS can vary in complexity from simple systems to advanced treatment solutions, depending on the size of the property and the volume of wastewater produced.

What This Means for Your Project

When planning a construction or renovation project in Colorado, understanding OWTS is essential for several reasons:

  • You may need to install an OWTS to manage wastewater effectively.
  • Permitting and regulatory compliance are crucial, as OWTS must meet state and local health codes.
  • Proper design and installation can prevent costly repairs and environmental issues in the future.
```
