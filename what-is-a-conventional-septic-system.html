What Is a Conventional Septic System?

By Kyle Rossignol, Licensed GC
```html

Quick Answer

A conventional septic system is an underground wastewater treatment system commonly used in rural areas where public sewage systems are unavailable. It consists of a septic tank that holds and treats wastewater, and a drain field that disperses the treated effluent into the soil. These systems are typically used for homes or businesses on large lots.

Detailed Explanation

A conventional septic system is designed to treat household wastewater through a simple yet effective process. It comprises two main components: the septic tank and the drain field (also known as a leach field). Here‚Äôs how it works:

  • Septic Tank: Wastewater from your home flows into the septic tank, which is a watertight container buried underground. The tank allows solids to settle at the bottom and form sludge while lighter materials, like grease, float to the top, forming scum. The middle layer, called effluent, is the liquid that exits the tank.
  • Drain Field: The effluent is then directed to the drain field, a series of perforated pipes buried in gravel or sand. Here, the effluent is further treated by soil microorganisms, which help purify the water before it eventually returns to the groundwater system.

Conventional septic systems are most suitable for locations with suitable soil and groundwater conditions. Here are some reasons why someone might need this type of system:

  • Location: If you live in a rural area where municipal sewage systems are not available, a conventional septic system is often the best option.
  • Property Size: Homes on larger lots with sufficient space for a septic system installation can benefit from this type of system.
  • Cost-Effective: Installing a conventional septic system can be less expensive in the long run compared to connecting to a public sewer.

It is essential to have the soil tested to determine if it can adequately absorb and treat wastewater, as not all properties are suitable for this type of system.

What This Means for Your Project

If you are planning to build a new home or make significant renovations in a Colorado area that lacks public sewer services, understanding conventional septic systems is vital. You will need to consider site assessments, permits, and installation costs. Proper planning and adherence to local regulations are crucial to ensure your septic system operates efficiently and safely.

Before proceeding with your project, consult with local health departments and professionals to determine the feasibility of a conventional septic system on your property. They can provide insights into soil testing and help you navigate the necessary permits.
```
