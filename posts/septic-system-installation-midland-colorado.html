Local Challenges in Teller County

Midland‚Äôs rugged terrain presents several challenges for septic installation. The predominant soil types include rocky granite, decomposed granite, and clay, which make standard septic systems less viable. Many properties require engineered systems due to these challenging conditions, as outlined in Colorado‚Äôs Regulation 43. Additionally, the water table is often deep, necessitating the use of cisterns for water supply. The frost line at elevations like Midland can reach 36-48 inches, further complicating installation. Seasonal weather patterns, including heavy snow loads and afternoon thunderstorms, also require strategic planning to ensure systems are winterized properly. Understanding these factors is crucial for effective onsite wastewater management.

Our Septic System Installation Process

At Tri-Lakes Contracting, our septic system installation process is thorough and efficient, ensuring compliance with local regulations and optimal system performance. The process begins with a detailed consultation to assess your property‚Äôs specific needs. This is followed by soil testing, where we dig two test holes, each eight feet deep, to analyze the soil's suitability for different septic systems. This information is then sent to a licensed engineer for a soils report, which guides our septic design.

Once the design is finalized, we assist you in obtaining the necessary septic permit from the Teller County Building Department, as all permits must go through this office. Following approval, the installation typically occurs from May to October, given the challenging winter access. Our team carefully excavates the site, installs the septic tank and components, and ensures proper connection to the leach field. After installation, we conduct a thorough septic inspection to ensure everything meets local standards. This meticulous approach ensures your septic system is not only compliant but also effective for the long term.

Cost Factors for Septic System Installation in Midland

The cost of septic installation in Midland varies significantly based on several factors. Based on our accurate pricing data, the total installed costs for an engineered septic system typically range from $35,000 to $50,000 for basic systems, while sand filter systems can range from $45,000 to $65,000. If an Advanced Treatment Unit (ATU) is required, the cost can rise to $55,000 to $80,000+. Additional costs may arise from site conditions such as rocky terrain, which can add $10,000 to $25,000 to the total price. It‚Äôs also essential to consider the tank sizing formula for cisterns, which is calculated based on the number of bedrooms (2 people per bedroom x 100 gallons/day).

Other factors influencing costs include the excavation needed for the system, which can range from $8,000 to $20,000+, and the required permits, which can vary from $500 to $2,000. Ultimately, the investment in a properly designed and installed septic system can save homeowners from future headaches and costly repairs.

Frequently Asked Questions About Septic System Installation

Q: How much does septic installation cost in Colorado?

A: The cost of septic installation in Colorado typically ranges from $35,000 to $80,000, depending on the type of system and site conditions. Factors such as rocky terrain and permit fees can add significantly to the overall cost.

Q: How long does septic installation take?

A: The timeline for septic installation usually spans from a few days to a couple of weeks, depending on the complexity of the system and site conditions. Delays can occur due to weather or permitting processes.

Q: Do I need an engineered septic system?

A: Most properties in the Midland area require engineered septic systems due to challenging soil conditions and regulations set forth in Colorado‚Äôs Regulation 43. These systems are designed to function effectively in the unique mountain terrain.

Q: What is a perc test?

A: In Colorado, we do not conduct traditional perc tests. Instead, we perform soil tests to assess the suitability of the land for septic systems. This involves digging test holes and analyzing the soil for drainage capabilities.
