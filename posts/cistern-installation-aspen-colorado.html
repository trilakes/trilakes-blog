Cost Factors for Cistern Installation in Aspen

When planning for water storage tank installation in Aspen, understanding the associated costs is vital. The total installed costs for a 5,000-gallon basic system generally range from $25,000 to $35,000, while a 10,000-gallon system can cost between $40,000 and $55,000. The sizing of your cistern is crucial; for a typical three-bedroom home, with an occupancy of six people, you would require a minimum of 600 gallons per day. Depending on your intended refill frequency, you might need a system capable of holding anywhere from 36,000 to 72,000 gallons to get through winter, raising your budget significantly.

Additional costs can arise from excavation, which can range from $3,000 to $8,000 depending on site access and rock conditions. Furthermore, the installation of the necessary pump system and pressure tank generally adds between $2,500 and $5,000 to your budget. It's important to note that rocky or difficult terrain can increase excavation costs by 20-40%, and remote access can also add to overall expenses, particularly during winter installations.

Frequently Asked Questions About Cistern Installation

Q: How much does a cistern cost in Colorado?

A: The cost of a cistern in Colorado varies based on size and installation conditions. A typical 5,000-gallon system starts at around $25,000 and can go up to $35,000, while a 10,000-gallon system often ranges from $40,000 to $55,000, with additional costs for excavation and pump systems.

Q: What size underground water storage tank do I need?

A: The size of your underground water storage tank should be based on your daily water usage, calculated as 100 gallons per person per day. For example, a three-bedroom home that accommodates six people would require a minimum tank size of 36,000 gallons to ensure adequate supply, especially in winter.

Q: How long does cistern installation take?

A: Typically, cistern installation can take from one to three weeks, depending on site conditions, permit acquisition, and the complexity of excavation. Weather can also impact the timeline, particularly in winter.

Q: Cistern vs well - which is better?

A: Choosing between a cistern and a well depends on individual circumstances. Cisterns are advantageous in areas with deep water tables or unreliable well sources. They also provide a backup water supply and can be essential for properties that experience limited winter access.
