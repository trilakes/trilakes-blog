Guide to Evaluating Buildable Land in Colorado

Not All Land is Buildable (At Least Not Affordably)

When it comes to purchasing land in the beautiful Colorado mountains, the allure of a stunning view lot can often cloud judgment regarding its buildability. Just because a piece of land is picturesque doesn't mean it's suitable for construction. Factors such as slope, soil conditions, and access can turn what looks like a dream location into a logistical nightmare.

To be classified as "buildable," a plot of land must meet specific criteria set by local zoning laws and regulations. These include adequate access, proper soil conditions, and the ability to connect to essential services like water and sewage. It's also crucial to differentiate between affordable buildability and what is technically possible; while some land may be buildable, the costs associated with making it functional can skyrocket. Thus, understanding what makes land truly buildable is essential for any prospective landowner.

Site Feasibility Factors

Slope & Terrain

The slope and terrain of a property are critical factors in determining buildability. Steep slopes, generally defined as greater than 30%, can lead to expensive foundation requirements, erosion issues, and complicated drainage systems. Additionally, rocky outcroppings can pose significant challenges during excavation, potentially leading to unanticipated costs. An ideal buildable lot should have a flat pad area that allows for easier construction and a solid foundation.

Soil Conditions

Soil conditions play a pivotal role in evaluating land for building. Rocky soil might require extensive excavation but can offer a stable foundation. Conversely, clay-rich soil can lead to drainage issues and may require a more complex foundation system due to its expansive nature. If you plan to install a septic system, a percolation (perc) test is essential to ensure the soil can adequately absorb wastewater. These tests can reveal significant information about the land's suitability for construction.

Access

Access to the property is another critical factor. Construction equipment needs to reach the site, and if the land is located on a remote road with limited access, it could complicate construction logistics. Year-round access is also vital to ensure that you can reach the site regardless of weather conditions. Additionally, evaluating the feasibility and cost of building a driveway to the property is essential. A well-planned access route can save time and money during construction.

Water

Water availability is a fundamental consideration when evaluating land for building. Assessing well feasibility involves understanding the depth of water tables in the area and the associated costs of drilling a well. In areas where well water is scarce, a cistern may serve as an alternative, but it comes with its own set of challenges and costs. Additionally, ensure that water hauling access is available if you're considering non-traditional water sources.

Septic

When it comes to waste management, septic systems are often necessary in rural areas. One of the first steps in evaluating land for septic is determining whether the land will perc. If the soil fails the perc test, you may need to explore engineered systems, which can be more expensive and complicated. It's also crucial to consider the required setbacks from wells and property lines, as these regulations often dictate where you can place a septic system.

Red Flags When Evaluating Land

While searching for buildable land, keep an eye out for potential red flags that could indicate issues down the line. Properties with very steep slopes (>30%) can lead to complicated and costly construction challenges. How to Evaluate Before Buying

Before committing to a land purchase, conducting thorough evaluations is crucial. Start by walking the property with a qualified contractor who can provide insights into potential construction challenges. Research local well depths to understand water availability and costs. Ask the current owners about previous perc tests to gauge soil suitability for septic systems. Checking county records for setbacks can also help ensure that your building plans comply with local regulations. Gathering this information before buying will enable you to make a more informed decision and avoid costly surprises.
