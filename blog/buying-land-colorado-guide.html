Why Due Diligence Matters

Buying raw mountain land in Colorado is excitingâ€”but it's also one of the easiest places to make expensive mistakes. We've seen buyers discover after closing that their "perfect" lot can't get a septic permit, has no legal road access, or would cost $200,000+ to develop.

The reality: Not all land is buildable. And land that IS buildable may have hidden costs that make the "cheap" lot very expensive. Thorough due diligence before purchase is the difference between a dream property and a nightmare investment.

This guide covers everything you need to investigate before buying mountain land in Colorado. Use it as your checklist, and consider hiring professionals for items you can't evaluate yourself.

Is This Land Buildable?

A lot being "for sale" doesn't mean you can build on it. Buildability requires:

  • Legal access â€“ Can you legally get to the property?
  • Septic viability â€“ Will the county approve waste disposal?
  • Water source â€“ Well, cistern, or community water?
  • Utility availability â€“ Electric, and possibly propane/internet
  • Proper zoning â€“ Is residential use allowed?
  • Physical feasibility â€“ Can you actually build on the terrain?

Missing any ONE of these can make land unbuildable or extremely expensive to develop.

Water: Wells, Cisterns & Rights

Water is often the most critical issue for Colorado mountain land. Unlike other states, Colorado's water rights are complexâ€”you can own land but not have the right to use water beneath it.

Well Permits

In many mountain areas, well permits are available for domestic use (household water). But some areas have over-allocated water, making new well permits unavailable. ALWAYS verify well permit availability before purchasing.

Check with: Colorado Division of Water Resources

Learn more: Colorado Well Permit Requirements | What If Well Permit Denied?

Cisterns (Hauled Water)

If well permits aren't available, most counties allow cisternsâ€”large underground water tanks filled by delivery trucks. This is a viable solution but requires:

  • Regular water deliveries ($150-$400/load)
  • Proper tank sizing (typically 2,500-10,000 gallons)
  • Year-round access for water trucks

Septic: Will the Land Perc?

No sewer = you need septic. And septic requires county approval based on soil testing.

The Perc Test

A percolation (perc) test measures how fast water drains through soil. This determines whether a conventional septic system will work. If soil drains too fast or too slow, you may need an engineered system (more expensive) or the land may be unbuildable.

Critical: Never buy land without knowing septic viability. Get a perc test OR written confirmation from the county about what system type will be approved.

Access: Legal & Physical

Access problems kill more land deals than any other issue. Verify BOTH legal and physical access.

Legal Access

You need documented, legally enforceable access to your property:

  • Deeded easement â€“ Recorded access right across neighboring land
  • County road frontage â€“ Direct access to public road
  • HOA roads â€“ Private roads maintained by association

Warning: "The neighbors let the previous owner drive through" is NOT legal access. Verify recorded easements with the title company.

Physical Access

Even with legal access, consider:

  • Is the road drivable year-round?
  • Who maintains it? (County, HOA, you?)
  • Will construction equipment fit?
  • Is winter access realistic?

Road Maintenance

Many mountain roads are private. Understand:

  • Who pays for maintenance?
  • Is there a road maintenance agreement?
  • What's the annual cost?
  • Who plows snow?

Internet & Phone

Remote mountain properties often lack wired internet. Options:

  • Starlink satellite ($120/month, good speeds)
  • LTE/5G fixed wireless (where available)
  • Legacy satellite (slow, high latency)

Propane

No natural gas in rural mountains. Propane delivery is standard but verify the property is accessible to delivery trucks.

Zoning, Setbacks & Restrictions

County Zoning

Verify the land is zoned for residential use. Check:

  • What can you build? (Single family, ADU, barn?)
  • Minimum lot size requirements
  • Maximum building height
  • Building setbacks from property lines

HOA Restrictions

Many mountain subdivisions have HOAs with rules about:

  • Minimum home size
  • Architectural style
  • Building materials and colors
  • Outbuildings and accessory structures
  • Construction timelines

Get the covenants in writing and read them carefully.

Deed Restrictions

Some properties have recorded restrictions separate from HOAs. These may limit building envelope, prohibit certain uses, or require specific materials.

For permit details, see our Colorado Permits Guide.

Terrain & Site Assessment

Slope

Building on slopes costs more:

  • 0-10% grade: Standard construction
  • 10-20% grade: Moderate extra cost, may need retaining walls
  • 20-30% grade: Significant extra cost, specialized foundation
  • 30%+ grade: Very difficult/expensive, may be unbuildable

Rock & Soil

Rocky terrain increases excavation costs dramatically. Granite bedrock near the surface can add $20,000-$50,000+ to site work.

Drainage & Wetlands

Watch for:

  • Streams or seasonal drainage
  • Wetland areas (regulated, can't build on)
  • Flood zones
  • High water table

Fire Danger

Most mountain areas are in wildfire zones. Consider:

  • Wildfire mitigation requirements (defensible space)
  • Insurance availability and cost
  • Evacuation routes

Common Mistakes to Avoid

We've seen buyers make these mistakes repeatedly:

1. Buying Without Perc Test

"It looks like good land" isn't enough. No perc = potential unbuildable land.

2. Assuming Access Exists

Driving across someone's property once doesn't create legal access. Verify recorded easements.

3. Underestimating Development Costs

Site development often costs $75,000-$150,000+. Budget for this BEFORE the land purchase.

4. Not Reading HOA Covenants

Covenants may prohibit your building type, require expensive finishes, or set unrealistic timelines.

5. Buying Based on Views Alone

Beautiful views don't make land buildable. The flattest, most buildable spot may face the wrong direction.

6. Skipping Professional Assessment

A $2,000 professional assessment can prevent a $100,000+ mistake.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if land is buildable in Colorado?Land is buildable if it has: legal access, viable septic options (passes perc test or engineered system possible), water source (well permit or cistern allowed), utility availability, proper zoning, and achievable building setbacks. Learn more.
What should I check before buying mountain land?Check: perc test for septic, well permit availability, legal access, utility access, zoning and setbacks, flood zones, fire danger, HOA restrictions, deed restrictions, easements, road maintenance agreements, and slope/terrain for buildability. Full checklist.
Can I build without a well in Colorado?Yes. If you can't get a well permit, you can typically use a cistern (water storage tank with hauled water). Many mountain properties in Colorado use cisterns as their primary water source. Learn more.
What if land doesn't perc for septic?If land fails a traditional perc test, you may still have options: engineered septic systems, aerobic treatment units, or alternative drain field designs. These cost more but can make otherwise unbuildable land viable. See options.
How much does land due diligence cost?

Basic due diligence (perc test, site visit, utility research) costs $1,500-$3,000. Comprehensive assessment (survey, soil boring, buildability analysis) runs $3,000-$6,000. This investment prevents $50,000+ mistakes.

Should I get a survey before buying?

Yes, especially for rural mountain land. Surveys verify boundaries, identify easements, and confirm buildable area. Cost: $500-$2,000 depending on lot size and complexity.

What questions should I ask the seller?Ask about: existing perc tests, well permits or water issues, known easements, road maintenance, HOA dues, utility availability, and any past building attempts. See full list.
