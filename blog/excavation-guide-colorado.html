What is Excavation & When Do You Need It?

Excavation is the foundation of every construction project in Colorado's mountains. Whether you're building a custom home, installing a septic system, or creating access to a remote property, excavation prepares your land for what comes next. In mountain terrain, excavation is more complex than flatland workâ€”dealing with rocky soil, steep grades, limited access, and environmental regulations requires specialized experience.

You'll need excavation services for:

  • Site Preparation: Clearing land, removing trees, and creating a level building pad
  • Foundation Work: Digging for basements, crawlspaces, or slab foundations
  • Driveway Construction: Building access roads through difficult terrain
  • Utility Installation: Trenching for water lines, septic systems, and electrical
  • Land Grading: Reshaping terrain for drainage and erosion control

For a complete overview of site prep work, see our Site Preparation & Earthwork Guide.

The Excavation Process Step-by-Step

Step 1: Site Assessment

Before any digging begins, a thorough site assessment identifies soil conditions, rock presence, drainage patterns, and access challenges. This determines what equipment is needed and realistic cost estimates. See our property assessment services.

Step 2: Permitting

Most excavation work in Colorado requires permits. Grading permits are typically needed for moving more than 50 cubic yards of soil. We handle permitting for all our clientsâ€”learn more in our Colorado Permits Guide.

Step 3: Clearing & Grubbing

Trees, brush, and stumps are removed. In fire-prone areas, this must meet wildfire mitigation requirements. Cleared material is either chipped on-site, hauled away, or burned (with permit).

Step 4: Rough Grading

The land is shaped to create level building areas and proper drainage flow. This is where major earthmoving happensâ€”cutting into hillsides, filling low spots, and creating the overall site layout.

Step 5: Excavation

The actual digging for foundations, driveways, or utilities. In rocky terrain, this may require hydraulic breakers or even blasting. For more on how long this takes, see our timeline guide.

Step 6: Fine Grading & Compaction

Final shaping and soil compaction prepares the site for the next phaseâ€”foundation pouring, driveway base, or septic installation.

Colorado Mountain Excavation Challenges

Excavation in Colorado's mountains isn't like flatland work. Here's what makes it different:

Rocky Terrain

Much of Colorado's mountain region sits on granite, decomposed granite, or other rock formations. Hitting rock changes everythingâ€”costs increase, timelines extend, and specialized equipment becomes necessary. Read about rock removal services.

Steep Grades

Building on slopes requires more cut-and-fill work, retaining walls, and erosion control. Steeper sites cost significantly more to develop.

Limited Access

Getting heavy equipment to remote mountain properties can be challenging. Sometimes access roads must be built before the main excavation can begin.

Short Building Season

At higher elevations, the excavation season runs roughly April through October. Frozen ground and deep snow limit winter work. See our fall excavation guide for seasonal considerations.

Environmental Regulations

Wetlands, streams, and steep slopes all have setback requirements. Your excavation contractor must understand these regulations. Learn about mountain-specific challenges in why mountain construction costs more.

How to Choose an Excavation Contractor

Choosing the right excavation contractor is critical. Here's what to look for:

  • Mountain Experience: Flatland contractors often struggle in mountain terrain. Ask specifically about their experience in your county.
  • Proper Equipment: Mountain excavation requires different equipmentâ€”track machines for steep terrain, rock breakers for granite.
  • Licensing & Insurance: Verify current liability insurance and contractor licensing.
  • Local Knowledge: A contractor who knows local permit requirements, soil conditions, and inspectors saves time and money.
  • Clear Estimates: Get detailed written estimates that specify what's included and what's extra (rock removal, hauling, etc.).

For more guidance, read our questions to ask an excavation contractor and how to choose the right contractor.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Unwilling to pull permits

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does excavation cost in Colorado?Excavation in Colorado typically costs $50-$200 per hour for equipment and operator, or $1,500-$5,000+ per day depending on scope. Mountain terrain, rock removal, and access difficulty can increase costs significantly. See our complete cost guide.
How long does excavation take?A typical residential excavation project takes 1-5 days for site prep, 2-7 days for foundation excavation, and 1-3 days for driveway work. Mountain terrain and weather can extend timelines. Learn more about timelines.
Do I need a permit for excavation in Colorado?

Yes, most excavation work requires permits. Grading permits are typically required for moving more than 50 cubic yards of soil. Foundation excavation requires building permits. Check your county requirements.

What if you hit rock during excavation?

Hitting rock is common in Colorado mountains. Soft rock can be broken with excavator-mounted hydraulic breakers. Hard granite may require specialized rock removal equipment or, in extreme cases, blasting (permitted separately). This adds costâ€”typically $50-$200 per cubic yard.

What is the best time of year for excavation?

Late spring through early fall (May-October) is ideal. Ground is thawed and workable, weather is more predictable, and you have time for follow-up construction before winter.

How do I prepare my property for excavation?

Mark property boundaries, identify utility locations, clear access for equipment, and have your site plan ready. We'll handle the rest during our site assessment.

What's included in site preparation?

Site preparation includes clearing vegetation, removing trees and stumps, grading and leveling, creating access roads, utility trenching, and preparing the building pad for foundation work.

Do you handle permits?

Yes. Tri-Lakes Contracting handles all permitting for our clients, including grading permits, building permits, and access permits as required by your county.
