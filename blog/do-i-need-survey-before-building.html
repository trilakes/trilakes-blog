Do I Need a Survey Before Building?

By Kyle Rossignol, Licensed GC
```html

Quick Answer

Yes, you typically need a survey before building in Colorado. A survey provides essential information about your property boundaries, easements, and site conditions, ensuring compliance with local regulations. Skipping this step can lead to costly mistakes, disputes with neighbors, and delays in your construction project.

Detailed Explanation

In Colorado, obtaining a land survey before starting your construction project is highly recommended, and in many cases, it is a requirement. A survey delineates your property‚Äôs boundaries and highlights important features such as easements, rights-of-way, and any encroachments. Having this information is crucial for several reasons:

  • Boundary Clarity: A survey confirms the exact boundaries of your property, preventing future disputes with neighbors.
  • Local Regulations: Many municipalities require a survey for building permits. Without one, your application may be denied.
  • Site Conditions: Surveys can identify topography, vegetation, and water drainage, all of which impact your construction plans.
  • Future Planning: Knowing your property lines helps in planning future expansions or additions without legal issues.

While not every project may require a full survey, certain signs indicate that you should definitely consider obtaining one:

  • You are building near property lines or existing structures.
  • Your property is in a developed area with complex boundary issues.
  • You are planning to subdivide your property.
  • There are existing disputes or uncertainties regarding property lines.

What This Means for Your Project

Failing to get a survey can result in significant setbacks, such as fines, required modifications to your plans, or even legal disputes with neighbors. It‚Äôs essential to address these issues upfront to ensure a smoother building process. If you decide to proceed without a survey, you may encounter unforeseen complications that could delay your project and increase costs.

Your next steps should include consulting with a local surveyor who understands the specific regulations in your area. They will provide the necessary documentation to support your building permit application and help you avoid pitfalls down the line.
```
