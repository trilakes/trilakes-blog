Understanding Colorado Building Permits

Building permits are required for most construction work in Colorado. They exist to ensure structures are safe, meet building codes, and comply with zoning regulations. For mountain property owners, permits often feel like an obstacleâ€”but they're actually protection for your investment.

In Colorado's mountain counties, permits also address unique concerns: wildfire mitigation, septic system safety, water resources, road access, and environmental protection. Understanding the permit landscape before you begin saves time, money, and frustration.

Types of Permits You May Need

Building Permits

Required for any new structure, addition, or significant renovation. This includes homes, garages, barns, and accessory dwelling units. The building permit covers structural work, electrical, plumbing, and mechanical systems.

Learn more: Building Permit Help

Septic/OWTS Permits

All on-site wastewater treatment systems (septic) require permits from the county health department. This includes conventional septic, aerobic systems, and advanced treatment units. The permit process includes:

  • Soil evaluation and perc testing
  • System design by licensed engineer
  • Site plan review
  • Installation inspections

Learn more: Do I Need a Septic Permit? | Septic Permit Help

Well Permits

Drilling a new water well requires a permit from the Colorado Division of Water Resources. The permit process ensures your well rights are valid and won't conflict with existing water rights in the area.

Learn more: Colorado Well Permit Requirements | Well Permit Denied?

Grading/Excavation Permits

Most counties require grading permits for moving significant amounts of soil (typically >50 cubic yards). This covers site preparation, foundation excavation, and driveway construction. See our Excavation Guide for details.

Access/Driveway Permits

If your driveway connects to a county road, you'll need an access permit specifying location, width, and drainage requirements. Some HOAs also require architectural approval for driveways.

Electrical Permits

Electrical work requires separate permits and inspections, typically handled by the state or county. This includes new service installation, panels, and major wiring work.

Fire Protection/Wildfire Mitigation

Many mountain counties require wildfire mitigation plans as part of the building permit. This includes defensible space clearing, fire-resistant materials, and water supply for firefighting.

The Permit Application Process

Step 1: Pre-Application Research

Before submitting anything, understand your property's zoning, setbacks, easements, and any deed restrictions. This prevents wasted time on projects that aren't allowed.

Step 2: Site Plan Development

Most permits require a site plan showing:

  • Property boundaries and dimensions
  • Proposed structure location with setbacks
  • Driveway and access points
  • Septic system location (if applicable)
  • Well location (if applicable)
  • Drainage patterns
  • Existing structures

Step 3: Construction Documents

For building permits, you'll need architectural plans including:

  • Floor plans with dimensions
  • Foundation plan
  • Roof plan
  • Elevations (all sides)
  • Structural details
  • Energy compliance documentation

Step 4: Submit Application

Submit your application package with all required documents and fees. Most counties now accept online submissions, though some still require in-person filing.

Step 5: Plan Review

County staff reviews your plans for code compliance. They may request changes or additional information. This is where most delays occur.

Step 6: Permit Issuance

Once approved, you'll receive your permit. Post it on site before starting work. The permit specifies required inspections.

Step 7: Inspections & Final

Schedule inspections at each stage. After final inspection approval, you'll receive a Certificate of Occupancy (for habitable structures).

Common Permit Issues & How to Avoid Them

Denied Permits

Permits get denied for reasons including:

  • Zoning violations (structure type not allowed)
  • Setback encroachments
  • Inadequate septic design
  • Water rights issues
  • Environmental concerns (wetlands, steep slopes)

Learn more: What to Do If Your Permit Is Denied

Incomplete Applications

The most common problem. Counties return incomplete applications, restarting the clock. Ensure you have all required documents before submitting.

Plan Corrections Required

If your plans don't meet code, you'll need to revise and resubmit. Common issues: inadequate egress, wrong materials for fire zones, structural undersizing.

Inspection Failures

Failed inspections require corrections and re-inspection. Work with an experienced contractor to avoid common inspection failures.

Required Inspections

Most building permits require these inspections:

Foundation Inspections

  • Footing inspection before concrete pour
  • Foundation wall inspection
  • Backfill inspection (sometimes)

Framing Inspection

  • Structural framing complete but before insulation/drywall
  • Includes window/door installation verification

Mechanical/Electrical/Plumbing (MEP)

  • Rough-in inspections before covering
  • Final inspections after fixtures installed

Insulation & Energy

  • Energy code compliance verification
  • Insulation installation inspection

Final Inspection

  • Complete building walkthrough
  • All systems operational
  • Safety features verified

Septic Inspections

  • Tank placement and depth
  • Drain field installation
  • Final cover and grading

What If I Don't Get a Permit?

Building without required permits is risky:

  • Fines: Counties can assess penalties, sometimes double the permit fee
  • Stop Work Orders: Construction halted until permits obtained
  • Demolition: In extreme cases, unpermitted work must be removed
  • Sale Problems: Unpermitted work complicates property sales and appraisals
  • Insurance Issues: Claims may be denied for unpermitted structures
  • Liability: You're personally liable if unpermitted work causes injury

Bottom line: The permit process can be frustrating, but the risks of skipping it far outweigh the hassle of doing it right.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to get a building permit in Colorado?

Building permit timelines vary by county. Teller County typically takes 2-4 weeks, Park County 2-6 weeks, and El Paso County 4-8 weeks. Complex projects or incomplete applications take longer.

Do I need a permit to build a septic system?Yes, all septic systems in Colorado require an OWTS (On-Site Wastewater Treatment System) permit from your county health department. This includes system design, perc tests, and inspections. Learn more.
How much does a building permit cost?

Building permit fees are typically based on project value, ranging from $500-$5,000+ for residential construction. Septic permits run $300-$800, and well permits are $50-$250.

What happens if I build without a permit?

Building without required permits can result in fines, mandatory demolition, inability to sell the property, insurance issues, and legal liability. Some counties offer amnesty programs for unpermitted work.

Do I need a permit for excavation or grading?

Most counties require grading permits for moving more than 50 cubic yards of soil. Foundation excavation typically falls under the building permit. Driveway construction may require access permits.

Can I get a permit for a tiny home?

Yes, but requirements vary. Most counties treat tiny homes as either RVs (limited placement options), accessory dwelling units (ADUs), or standard dwellings depending on foundation type and size. Check your county's specific rules.

Do I need a permit to drill a well?Yes, drilling a water well in Colorado requires a permit from the Colorado Division of Water Resources. Your well driller typically handles this application. See requirements.
What if my permit application is denied?You can appeal permit denials or modify your plans to address the issues. Common solutions include design changes, variance applications, or working with the building department to find alternatives. Learn more.
