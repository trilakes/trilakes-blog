Local Challenges in Teller County

Cripple Creek's stunning scenery is matched by its challenging soil conditions, characterized by rocky granite, decomposed granite, and clay in the valleys. Most properties in this area require engineered septic systems due to these tough soil types, which can significantly complicate septic installation. Additionally, the water table tends to be deep, often requiring homeowners to seek alternative water sources like cisterns or water hauling. With a frost line of 36-48 inches, proper winterization of your septic system is essential to prevent freezing and damage during the colder months. Access can also be a concern, as many properties feature steep driveways that can limit access during winter storms. Understanding these challenges is vital for ensuring your onsite wastewater system operates effectively year-round.

Our Septic System Installation Process

At Tri-Lakes Contracting, we follow a detailed, step-by-step process to ensure that your septic system is installed correctly and efficiently. Initially, we conduct comprehensive soil testing using our mini excavator to dig two test holes, each 8 feet deep; this provides crucial data on soil composition, which is necessary for determining the appropriate system type. After the soils report is completed by a licensed engineer, we will help you apply for the required septic permit through the Teller County Building Department.

Once permits are secured, we schedule the excavation and installation, which typically occurs between May and October, taking full advantage of the building season. The installation process includes setting the septic tank and any necessary components, followed by establishing the leach field or drain field, depending on the engineered system required. After installation, we conduct a thorough septic inspection to ensure everything meets local regulations and functions as intended. Homeowners can generally expect the entire process‚Äîfrom initial consultation to final inspection‚Äîto take several weeks, depending on site conditions and permitting timelines.

Cost Factors for Septic System Installation in Cripple Creek

The cost of septic installation in Cripple Creek can vary widely based on a variety of factors, including soil conditions, access to the site, and the specific type of system required. For most properties, the installation of an engineered system‚Äîrequired due to the challenging mountain soils‚Äîtypically starts from $35,000 and can go up to $80,000 or more depending on complexity and site conditions. The costs break down as follows:

  • Soils test + engineering: $2,000-$4,000
  • Septic permits: $500-$2,000
  • Excavation for system: $8,000-$20,000+
  • Tank + components: $5,000-$15,000
  • Advanced Treatment Unit (ATU): $8,000-$15,000 (if required)
  • Electrical/controls: $2,000-$4,000

Additionally, if your property has rocky terrain or difficult access, you can expect to add $10,000 to $25,000 to the high-end estimates. For those considering a cistern, remember that the size should be based on your bedroom count‚Äîcalculating approximately 100 gallons per person per day. For example, a three-bedroom home would require a cistern capable of holding at least 600 gallons.

Frequently Asked Questions About Septic System Installation

Q: How much does septic installation cost in Colorado?

A: The cost of septic installation in Colorado typically ranges from $35,000 to $80,000, depending on soil conditions, system type, and site accessibility. For most properties in Cripple Creek, engineered systems are required, which tends to be on the higher end of this spectrum.

Q: How long does septic installation take?

A: The entire process of septic installation can take several weeks, depending on factors such as permitting timelines, soil testing, and weather conditions. Most installations occur during the building season from May to October in Teller County.

Q: Do I need an engineered septic system?

A: Most properties in Cripple Creek require engineered systems due to challenging soil conditions. A soils report will determine the necessity of an engineered system as mandated by Colorado's Regulation 43.

Q: What is a perc test?

A: In Colorado, we conduct soils tests instead of traditional perc tests. This involves digging two test holes to analyze the soil's drainage capabilities, helping us determine the right septic system for your property.
