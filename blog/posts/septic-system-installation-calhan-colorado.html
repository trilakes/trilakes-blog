Local Challenges in El Paso County

Calhan's landscape is characterized by rocky granite and decomposed granite soils, making septic system installation a complex task. Additionally, the water table tends to be deep in this region, which often requires homeowners to consider cisterns or water hauling as alternatives. With steep driveways and limited access during winter months, it's crucial to engage a septic contractor who understands these challenges and can navigate them effectively. Our Septic System Installation Process

At Tri-Lakes Contracting, we follow a detailed process to ensure your septic system is installed correctly and efficiently. First, we conduct comprehensive soil testing to determine the best plan for your property. This involves bringing in a mini excavator, digging two test holes at least eight feet deep, and sending soil samples to a licensed engineer for analysis. Once we receive the soils report, we can design an engineered system that meets your specific needs.

Next, we assist you in obtaining the necessary septic permit from the El Paso County Building Department. This is a crucial step, as every installation must comply with Colorado‚Äôs OWTS regulations outlined in Regulation 43. After securing the permit, we prepare your site by excavating for the system, which can take anywhere from a few days to several weeks depending on site conditions.

Finally, our team will install the septic tank, leach field, and any other components necessary for an effective system. With a clear understanding of the local environment, we ensure that everything is built to withstand the unique challenges posed by Calhan‚Äôs high altitude and rugged terrain. We also provide guidance on winterizing your new system to protect against freeze damage during the colder months.

Cost Factors for Septic System Installation in Calhan

The cost of installing a septic system in Calhan can vary significantly depending on several factors. Typically, homeowners can expect the total installed cost for a basic engineered system to range from $35,000 to $50,000. If you require a sand filter system, costs can go up to $45,000 to $65,000, while advanced treatment units (ATUs) can range from $55,000 to $80,000 or more, especially for properties with challenging soil conditions.

When planning for a cistern, remember to size it based on the number of bedrooms in your home; the standard calculation is 100 gallons per day for each bedroom, multiplied by two occupants per bedroom. For example, a three-bedroom home would require a 600-gallon cistern.

Additional factors that may increase the installation cost include rocky terrain which could add $10,000 to $25,000 to excavation expenses, the complexity of the leach field design, and necessary permits, which can range from $500 to $2,000 based on El Paso County regulations. Therefore, it is essential to engage a septic contractor who can provide a detailed estimate tailored to your property's unique conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions About Septic System Installation

Q: How much does septic installation cost in Colorado?

A: The cost of septic installation in Colorado varies significantly based on site conditions and the type of system required. For a basic engineered system, you can expect to pay between $35,000 and $50,000, while advanced treatment options may cost upwards of $55,000 to $80,000.

Q: How long does septic installation take?

A: The timeline for septic installation can range from a few days to several weeks, depending on factors such as the complexity of the system, weather conditions, and site accessibility. A thorough soils test and obtaining the necessary permits can add to the timeline as well.

Q: Do I need an engineered septic system?

A: Most properties in the Colorado mountains, including Calhan, require engineered septic systems due to challenging soil conditions. A soils report will help determine the best system type for your property to ensure proper functionality and compliance with local regulations.

Q: What is a perc test?

A: In Colorado, we perform soil testing instead of traditional perc tests. This involves digging two test holes to assess soil characteristics, which helps determine the appropriate septic system design for your property.
