Concrete vs Plastic Septic Tanks: Which is Better for Colorado?

By Kyle Rossignol
```html

Tank Material Matters

When it comes to selecting a septic tank for your property in Colorado, the choice of material is crucial. The two main options available are concrete and plastic (polyethylene) septic tanks. Both of these materials are effective in treating wastewater, but they come with different trade-offs that can impact their performance, installation, and lifespan. Understanding the differences between concrete and plastic septic tanks can help you make an informed decision that best suits your property and local conditions.

Concrete Septic Tanks

Pros:

  • Extremely durable: Concrete septic tanks have a lifespan of over 50 years, making them a long-term investment for wastewater management.
  • Heavy construction: Their weight prevents them from floating in high water table conditions, ensuring stability and functionality.
  • Resilient to soil pressure: Concrete tanks are robust and cannot be easily crushed by soil pressure, which is essential for maintaining structural integrity.
  • Familiarity: Most installers are well-acquainted with concrete tanks, making installation generally more straightforward and efficient.

Cons:

  • Heavy and cumbersome: The significant weight of concrete tanks can complicate delivery to remote locations, which may require special equipment or extended labor.
  • Cracking potential: If not installed correctly, concrete tanks can crack during freeze-thaw cycles, leading to potential leaks and costly repairs.
  • Transportation costs: Concrete tanks are more expensive to transport due to their weight, impacting the overall cost of installation.

Plastic (Poly) Septic Tanks

Pros:

  • Lightweight: Plastic septic tanks are significantly lighter than their concrete counterparts, making them easier to deliver to mountain sites and reducing transportation costs.
  • Freeze-thaw resistant: Unlike concrete, plastic tanks won't crack in response to freeze-thaw cycles, reducing the risk of damage in cold climates.
  • Corrosion resistant: Plastic is inherently resistant to corrosion, which can be beneficial for maintaining tank integrity over time.
  • Cost-effective: Generally, plastic tanks tend to be less expensive than concrete tanks, making them an attractive option for budget-conscious homeowners.

Cons:

  • Floating risk: One of the major concerns with plastic tanks is that they can float if the groundwater table rises. Proper anchoring is essential to prevent this issue.
  • Crushability: If backfilled improperly, plastic tanks can be crushed under soil pressure, potentially leading to system failure.
  • Shorter track record: Compared to concrete tanks, plastic tanks have a shorter history in the field, leading to uncertainties about long-term performance.

For Colorado Mountains: Our Recommendation

In the mountainous regions of Colorado, concrete septic tanks are generally preferred due to their durability and stability. The rocky terrain can actually enhance drainage, reducing the risk of water accumulation around the tank. While both types of tanks have their merits, the long-lasting nature of concrete and its resistance to floating in high water tables make it a more reliable option for homeowners in these areas. However, site-specific factors such as soil composition, elevation, and local regulations can also influence the best choice for your property. Consulting with a local septic system professional can help you evaluate these factors and make the right decision.

Installation Considerations

When installing a septic tank, several considerations come into play, particularly regarding access for delivery and excavation requirements. Concrete tanks, due to their weight, may require larger vehicles for delivery, and the installation process can demand more extensive excavation efforts. On the other hand, plastic tanks may be easier to maneuver but still require proper access to ensure a smooth installation. Additionally, the depth and type of soil at your site can influence the excavation process, affecting the overall installation timeline.
```
