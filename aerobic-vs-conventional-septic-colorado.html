Aerobic vs Conventional Septic Systems: Colorado Comparison

Two Types of Septic Systems

When it comes to wastewater management in rural areas of Colorado, septic systems are essential. The two primary types of septic systems are Conventional (anaerobic) systems and Aerobic Treatment Units (ATUs). Conventional septic systems, the most common choice, rely on natural processes to break down waste without the addition of oxygen. Wastewater flows into a septic tank, where solids settle at the bottom, forming sludge, while lighter materials float to the top, creating scum. The liquid effluent then flows into a drain field, where it is filtered through soil.

Aerobic Treatment Units, on the other hand, utilize oxygen to accelerate the decomposition of waste. These systems introduce air into the treatment process, which allows aerobic bacteria to thrive and break down organic matter more efficiently. As a result, ATUs produce a cleaner effluent that can be safely dispersed into a smaller drain field, making them suitable for sites with poor soil conditions. Understanding the differences between these two systems is crucial for homeowners in Colorado when choosing the right septic solution.

Conventional Septic Systems

Conventional septic systems consist of a septic tank and a drain field. When wastewater enters the septic tank, solids settle to the bottom, while grease and lighter particles rise to the top. The bacteria in the tank break down the solids anaerobically, and the liquid effluent flows into the drain field, where it is naturally filtered by the surrounding soil.

One of the primary advantages of conventional systems is their simplicity and fewer components, which often translates to lower installation costs, typically ranging from $12,000 to $25,000. Additionally, they require less maintenance, with pumping needed every 3-5 years, making them a cost-effective choice for many homeowners.

However, conventional systems do have their drawbacks. They require good soil percolation to function effectively; if the soil does not drain well, the system can fail, leading to potential health hazards and costly repairs. Furthermore, conventional systems necessitate a larger drain field, which may not be feasible on smaller properties. Overall, while conventional septic systems are an excellent option in many scenarios, they do have limitations that homeowners must consider.

Aerobic Treatment Units (ATU)

Aerobic Treatment Units are designed to enhance the wastewater treatment process by introducing oxygen into the system. This process supports the growth of aerobic bacteria, which break down waste more efficiently than anaerobic bacteria. In an ATU, wastewater is exposed to air through a blower, promoting faster decomposition and resulting in cleaner effluent.

One of the significant advantages of ATUs is their ability to produce cleaner effluent, which can be advantageous for properties with limited land area for drain fields. Since aerobic systems can work effectively in smaller areas, they are often required when soil conditions do not permit adequate percolation. However, the cost of installing an ATU can be higher, typically ranging from $18,000 to $40,000, due to the additional components and technology involved.

While ATUs offer many benefits, they also come with higher maintenance requirements. Homeowners must ensure that the system is regularly inspected (often quarterly) and maintain the mechanical components such as blowers and pumps. Additionally, ATUs require a consistent electricity supply, which can be a concern in remote areas. Thus, while an ATU may be the right choice for specific situations, the higher costs and maintenance needs are essential factors to consider.

When comparing the maintenance needs of conventional septic systems and aerobic treatment units, it's clear that they differ significantly. Conventional systems typically require less frequent maintenance, with pumping needed every 3-5 years, at a cost of approximately $300 to $500 per service. The simplicity of these systems results in fewer components that need monitoring, making them easier for homeowners to manage.

In contrast, aerobic treatment units involve more complex maintenance. Homeowners need to schedule quarterly inspections to ensure all components are functioning correctly, which can cost between $150 and $300 per quarter. The presence of mechanical parts, such as blowers and pumps, necessitates regular attention to prevent system failures. While ATUs can offer advantages in terms of effluent quality and efficiency, the additional maintenance requirements must be weighed against their benefits.

Which Do You Need?

If you're considering installing a septic system in Colorado, the first step is to conduct a percolation test to assess your soil's drainage capabilities. This test will inform whether a conventional or aerobic system is suitable for your property. Additionally, local county regulations will specify which type of system is required based on environmental considerations and soil conditions.
